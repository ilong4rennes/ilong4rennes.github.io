36200 Reasoning with Data - Notes

Author: Amanda Lu Date: July 29, 2022  6:09:16 Category: Statistics

Lecture 1: Background and Elements of Statistical Thinking

Three common aspects in data science

  • Sampling and study design (“Design”) - 3rd week
  • Exploratory data analysis, ‘EDA’ (“Description” / descriptive statistics) - 1st-2nd week + project 1
  • Inference (“Decide” / “Deduce”) - last half of the course + project 2

 

Probability and distributions

  • The body of theory that makes formal inference possible.

 

The ‘statistical perspective’

  • We learn truths about the world through data.
  • But variation in data is omnipresent, unavoidable, even natural.
  • However, if the source of variation is random, then the variation can be minimized with sample size, modeled with distributions, and measured with probability and margins of error.
    • Must include some assessment of the variation
    • Between individuals: standard deviation
    • Between groups: standard error
    • Between one sample and the population: probability / margin of error
  • Therefore, good scientific studies intentionally impose randomness, through the random selection of the people or things, and randomized assignment of the experimental treatment.
  • Random selection is why a study can be generalized to the world at large.
  • Randomized assignment of the experimental treatment is why a cause can be deduced for an observed effect.
    • Correlation isn’t causation.
  • Data analysis involves many series of managed choices and judgements.

 

Lecture 2: The Big Picture of Elementary Inference; Identifying Data Types; and EDA for univariate categorical data

The Big Picture of Inference

  • Population: the complete set of people or objects of interest
  • Sample: the subset of the population for which data can actually be obtained in a study
  • Parameter: a fixed number summarizing some feature of a population
    • usually unknown, since an entire population can usually never be measured
  • Statistic: a computed number that summarizes sample data in some appropriate way, which estimates a parameter.
    • Statistic is the best estimator of the corresponding parameter
    • Statistic varies from sample to sample, hence it’s an imperfect estimator of the parameter
    • If the source of the variation of the statistic is random, then the statistic has a probability distribution.
  • Inference: specifying the estimate of an unknown parameter (i.e., giving the estimate along with some measure of how far off the estimate is)

 

Identifying data types

  • Individuals (or Units): the people or objects described in the dataset
  • Variables: a characteristic which varies from person to person. It can typically be identified by a column in a spreadsheet. The name of the variable is identified by the column name.
    • Sex, age, weight…
  • Quantitative: naturally numerical
  • Categorical (or Qualitative): not naturally numerical

 

Representations of the distribution of one categorical variable

  • Frequency table for categorical data
    • Distribution: any representation of data showing what values the variables take and how often each such value appears.
  • Percent / Relative frequency
  • Frequency bar graph for categorical data
  • Percentage frequency bar graph for categorical data
  • Pie graph for categorical data
